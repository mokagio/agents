## Editorial overview

This draft argues that remote work is superior to office work and that companies should stop requiring office attendance. The problem is not style first; it is that the argument is built from universal claims, unsupported premises, and a conclusion that is much broader than the reasoning provided.

## Logic and reasoning

**Issues**

- "Remote work is obviously better than office work for everyone." This is an immediate overreach. "Everyone" makes the claim false on its face because many jobs cannot be done remotely, and even within knowledge work there are clear tradeoffs.
- "People get interrupted less at home, which means they are more productive" treats a disputed premise as settled fact and then turns it into a causal conclusion. Fewer interruptions do not automatically mean higher-value output.
- "so companies should stop requiring offices" does not follow from the prior sentence. Even if some workers are more productive at home, that does not establish that offices have no legitimate role.
- "teams can just use chat and video calls for everything and that solves collaboration" collapses collaboration into communications tooling. Coordination, trust-building, onboarding, conflict resolution, and creative work are not all solved by having chat and video.
- "companies that disagree are probably just old fashioned" substitutes dismissal for engagement. It avoids the strongest objections instead of answering them.

**Suggestions**

- Replace the universal thesis with a narrower claim that could actually be defended.
- Add at least one serious counterargument and answer it directly.
- Separate claims about focus, productivity, collaboration, and company policy instead of treating them as interchangeable.

## Structure and flow

**Issues**

- The piece has no real progression. It states a thesis, adds a few assertions, and ends with a dismissal of disagreement.
- "This is important because the future of work is changing" is filler. It gestures at significance without adding content or support.
- The ending weakens the piece. Closing on "old fashioned" makes the argument sound reactive rather than reasoned.

**Suggestions**

- Rebuild the draft around a simple structure: claim, evidence, counterargument, conclusion.
- Either remove the "future of work" sentence or replace it with a concrete point that advances the argument.
- End by restating the limited conclusion the evidence can support, not by attacking dissenters.

## Typos and grammar

## Targeted rewrite suggestions

Instead of "Remote work is obviously better than office work for everyone," use something like:

> For many knowledge workers, remote work can provide longer stretches of uninterrupted time than office work.

That version is narrower, defensible, and gives the rest of the piece somewhere real to go.
