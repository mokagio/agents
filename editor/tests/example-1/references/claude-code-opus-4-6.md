## Editorial overview

This draft argues for remote work superiority but relies on unsupported assertions and logical shortcuts.
Every claim is stated as self-evident rather than argued.
The piece needs substantial work on reasoning before any structural or stylistic polish matters.

## Logic and reasoning

**Issues**

- "obviously better than office work for everyone" — sweeping universal claim with no evidence. "Everyone" includes roles where remote work is impractical (surgery, manufacturing, lab work). This undermines credibility from the first sentence.
- "People get interrupted less at home" — presented as fact, but contestable. Home environments have their own interruptions (children, deliveries, household tasks). Even if true on average, the leap to "more productive" skips over the difference between fewer interruptions and better output.
- "companies should stop requiring offices" — the conclusion doesn't follow from the single premise (fewer interruptions). Offices serve purposes beyond heads-down productivity: onboarding, mentorship, spontaneous collaboration, culture-building. None of these are addressed.
- "chat and video calls… solves collaboration" — asserted without support. Async text and video calls have well-documented failure modes (context loss, meeting fatigue, timezone friction). Claiming they "solve" collaboration is exactly the kind of claim that needs evidence.
- "companies that disagree are probably just old fashioned" — dismisses all counterarguments as cultural backwardness. This is an ad hominem against organizations rather than an engagement with their reasons.

**Suggestions**

- Pick a narrower, defensible claim (e.g., "For knowledge workers who do primarily independent work, remote arrangements can increase deep-work time").
- Engage with at least one strong counterargument and respond to it. The piece currently has zero acknowledgment that reasonable people disagree for substantive reasons.
- Replace "obviously" and "just" with actual reasoning.

## Structure and flow

**Issues**

- No discernible structure — five sentences, each making a different claim, none developed. It reads as a list of opinions rather than an argument.
- "This is important because the future of work is changing" — a filler sentence that adds no information. What specifically is changing, and why does it support the thesis?

**Suggestions**

- Decide on one core claim and build the piece around it: state the claim, present evidence, address a counterargument, conclude.
- Cut the "future of work" sentence entirely or replace it with a concrete observation.

## Targeted rewrite suggestions

The opening sentence could be rewritten to make a claim worth arguing:

> Remote work gives knowledge workers more uninterrupted time for deep work than a typical open-plan office does.

This is narrower, more specific, and actually defensible — a starting point for a real argument rather than a blanket declaration.
